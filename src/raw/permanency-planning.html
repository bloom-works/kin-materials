Our primary goal is for children and youth in DCYF’s custody to return home. If a child or youth cannot safely return to their parents, we may explore guardianship or adoption.  {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for caregivers of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on permanency planning. {% endCallout %} ## How planning for permanency works Permanency planning starts as soon as DCYF places a child or youth in out-of-home care and continues until a child or youth is no longer in DCYF’s care. The caseworker discusses permanency regularly with the family, the child or youth and their caregivers. DCYF must establish a permanency plan within 60 days of the child or youth’s first placement. Every six months, there will be a Youth and Family meeting to discuss permanency planning with the parents, caregivers, and other people involved in the child or youth's care. If the youth is 12 or older, they can also attend the meeting.  After about 12 months in foster care, the child or youth will have a permanency plan hearing. The caseworker will propose a permanent plan in their court report. The court will decide what the child or youth’s permanent plan should be. There will be court hearings every six months to discuss permanency until the child or youth is in a safe, and permanent living situation. The plan may change in future court hearings if the child or youth’s situation changes. As the child or youth’s caregiver, you have the right to attend court hearings. It’s the responsibility of the caseworker or local office staff to let you know about upcoming court dates. If you do not hear about court dates, talk to the caseworker. If the caseworker still does not provide you with court dates, talk to the caseworker’s supervisor. The Guardian Ad Litem (GAL) or the child or youth’s attorney can also provide you with dates of upcoming hearings. ## Permanency outcomes There are four possible options for children and youth in foster care.  * Reunification  * Guardianship  * Adoption  * Another Planned Permanent LIving Arrangement (for ages 16 and up) Keep reading this section for more information about each permanency outcome.   [DCYF’s Permanency Planning Matrix guide](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0088.pdf) has an in-depth comparison of these outcomes, plus the legal requirements, and the services available for parents, guardians, and adoptive parents.  ### Reunification Reunification is when a child goes home. Parents have full parental rights and responsibility for their child. The parents’ legal relationship with their child stays the same. ### Guardianship Under a guardianship, a legal guardian takes over responsibility for caring and making decisions  for the child or youth until they turn 18. The parents are still their legal parents on paper. Children and youth who are legally free (this means that a judge has terminated their parents’ rights) can also enter into guardianship.  If you’re a licensed kinship caregiver or foster parent, you’ll stop getting monthly foster care payments when you become the child or youth’s legal guardian. But you may be eligible for other payments through DCYF’s Guardianship Assistance Program.  Learn more about guardianship (add link) [Learn more about DCYF's Guardianship Assistance program](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0128.pdf) ### Adoption An adoption is when a court terminates the parent’s rights, and creates a new legal relationship between the child or youth and an adoptive parent. The adoptive parent has all care, custody, and legal rights to make decisions for the child or youth. The child or youth has the legal right to inherit from their adoptive parent and the adoptive parent’s relatives.  If you’re a licensed kinship caregiver or foster parent, you’ll stop getting monthly foster care payments when a court finalizes the adoption. But if the child or youth has special needs, you may be eligible for other payments through DCYF’s Adoption Support Program.  [Learn more about adoption](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/adoption) [Learn more about DCYF's Adoption Support program](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/adoption-support-program) ### Another Planned Permanent Living Arrangement, or APPLA (also called long-term kinship or foster care)  Sometimes, when a youth who is 16 or older, the youth, their caseworker, their caregiver, and their family may decide that none of the legal permanency options are in the youth’s best interest (for example, if both the youth and their caregiver do not want to pursue guardianship or adoption).  APPLA (also called long-term kinship or foster care) is an alternative to guardianship and adoption for youth ages 16 to 18. Under APPLA, a youth ages 16 to 18 can stay with a foster parent if both the youth and the foster parent agree. Unlike guardianship and adoption, APPLA is not legally binding. It can end if the youth, or the foster parent or kinship caregiver no longer want to participate.  Learn more about APPLA (add link)
