Our primary goal is for children and youth in DCYF’s custody to return home. If a child or youth cannot safely return to their parents, we may explore guardianship or adoption. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for caregivers of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only. Individual Tribes may have different rules for permanency planning for children and youth in the care of a Tribe. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on permanency planning. {% endCallout %} ## How planning for permanency works ### Youth and Family meetings Permanency planning starts as soon as DCYF places a child or youth in out-of-home care and continues until a child or youth is no longer in DCYF’s care. You, the caregiver, the caseworker, and the child or youth’s family will discuss permanency regularly at Youth and Family meetings (formerly known as shared planning meetings). These meetings are team meetings where everyone involved in a child or youth's life comes together to talk, share information, and build or update the case plan. These meetings are collaborative. Every six months, there will be a Youth and Family meeting specifically to discuss permanency planning with you, the family, and other people involved in the child or youth's care. If the youth is 12 or older, they can also attend the meeting. ### Permanency plans DCYF must establish a permanency plan within 60 days of the child or youth’s first placement. While our primary goal is reunification, the law requires us to also work towards a backup permanency outcome for the child or youth (also called a concurrent plan) in case reunification is not possible.  Keep reading this page for more information about permanency outcomes. ### Court hearings After about 12 months in foster care, the child or youth will have a permanency plan hearing. The caseworker will propose a permanent plan in their court report. The court will decide what the child or youth’s permanent plan should be. There will be court hearings every six months to discuss permanency until the child or youth is in a safe, and permanent living situation. The plan may change in future court hearings if the child or youth’s situation changes. As the child or youth’s caregiver, you have the right to attend court hearings. It’s the responsibility of the caseworker or local office staff to let you know about upcoming court dates. If you do not hear about court dates, talk to the caseworker or the caseworker’s supervisor. If the child or youth has an attorney, CASA (court-appointed special advocate), or guardian ad litem (GAL), they can also provide you with dates of upcoming hearings. (A “guardian ad litem” is someone the court appoints to represent the best interests of a child. A GAL is different from a guardian.)   If you cannot attend a court hearing, you can still share your thoughts with the court. Fill out the [Caregivers’ Report to the Court form](<>) and submit it to the caseworker two to four weeks before the court hearing. ## Permanency outcomes There are four possible options for children and youth in foster care.  * Reunification  * Guardianship  * Adoption  * Another Planned Permanent LIving Arrangement (for ages 16 and up) [DCYF’s Permanency Planning Matrix guide](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0088.pdf) compares the outcomes, plus the legal requirements, and the services available for parents, guardians, and adoptive parents. ### Reunification Reunification is when a child goes home. Parents have full parental rights and responsibility for their child. The parents’ legal relationship with their child stays the same. ### Guardianship Under a guardianship, a legal guardian takes over responsibility for caring and making decisions  for the child or youth until they turn 18. Foster parents, kinship caregivers, and other people can serve as guardians.  Usually, the parents are still their legal parents on paper. For example, the child or youth keeps the legal right to inherit from the parents. The parents may also keep the right to visit the child or youth, depending on the court’s visitation plan. This means the parents can still maintain and build their relationship with the child or youth. And in rare situations, it’s possible for parents to regain custody of the child or youth who is in a guardianship, if the guardian agrees.  Other times, children and youth can enter a guardianship after the parents’ rights have been terminated. In these cases, the parents do not have contact with the children and youth, and they cannot regain custody.  If you’re a licensed kinship caregiver or foster parent, you’ll stop getting monthly foster care payments when you become the child or youth’s legal guardian. But you may be eligible for other payments through [DCYF’s Guardianship Assistance program](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0128.pdf). Learn more about guardianship [**add link**] or read [DCYF’s Guardianship and Adoption Comparison Guide](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0117.pdf). ### Adoption An adoption is when a court terminates the parent’s rights, and creates a new legal relationship between the child or youth and an adoptive parent. The adoptive parent has all care, custody, and legal rights to make decisions for the child or youth. The child or youth has the legal right to inherit from their adoptive parent and the adoptive parent’s relatives.  In a closed adoption, biological parents have little to no contact with the child or youth after the adoption. In an open adoption, the level of contact depends on what the child or youth, and the biological and adoptive parents are comfortable with.  It’s almost impossible for parents to regain custody of a child or youth who has been adopted.  If you’re a licensed kinship caregiver or foster parent, you’ll stop getting monthly foster care payments when a court finalizes the adoption. But if the child or youth has special needs, you may be eligible for other payments through [DCYF’s Adoption Support program](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/adoption-support-program). Learn more about [adoption](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/adoption) or read [DCYF’s Guardianship and Adoption Comparison Guide](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0117.pdf). ### Another Planned Permanent Living Arrangement, or APPLA (also called long-term kinship or foster care)  Sometimes, when a youth who is 16 or older, the youth, their caseworker, their caregiver, and their family may decide that none of the legal permanency options are in the youth’s best interest (for example, if both the youth and their caregiver do not want to pursue guardianship or adoption).  APPLA (also called long-term kinship or foster care) is an alternative to guardianship and adoption for youth ages 16 to 18. Under APPLA, a youth ages 16 to 18 can stay with a foster parent if [the youth, their caregiver, and their parents sign a written agreement](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/forms/15-322.pdf). Unlike guardianship and adoption, APPLA is not legally binding. It can end if the youth, or the foster parent or kinship caregiver no longer want to participate.  If you or the youth want to know more about APPLA, talk to the caseworker.
