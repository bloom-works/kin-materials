Guardianship and adoption are more permanent kinds of caregiving that happen outside the foster care system. Learn more about guardianship and adoption [add link]. {% endCallout %} We encourage you to think about foster care as a system of support for families. As a caregiver, you are part of this support network and can make a difference in the lives of young people and their parents, too. There are several ways to be a caregiver for a child or youth [add jump links]: * Kinship caregiving * Foster parenting, including Therapeutic Foster Care * Voluntary placement agreement (VPA) * Respite care If you are a full-time caregiver looking for substitute care, learn more about respite care and other breaks from caregiving [add link]. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} The information on this page may not apply to caregivers of children and youth who are connected to Tribes. Individual Tribes have different requirements for caregivers of children and youth in the custody of a Tribe. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information. {% endCallout %} ## Kinship caregiving Kinship caregiving is a kind of foster care. In kinship care, children or youth are cared for by relatives and suitable others. "Suitable others" refers to unrelated kin or close family friends. While it is often provided by grandparents, kinship care includes many other relationships, such as adult siblings, "bonus" aunties and uncles, neighbors, and more. If you’re the full-time caregiver for a child or youth who is related to you or who you knew before you became their caregiver, you are likely a kinship caregiver.  Kinship caregivers get the same kinds of support as foster parents. **You do not need a license to be a kinship caregiver, but getting licensed means that you can get paid for caregiving.** Getting licensed involves having your home checked to make sure it is safe for the child or youth. [Learn more about the home study process](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/home-study-process). Benefits of getting licensed include: * Monthly payments [add link], including for caring for youth over 18  who choose to participate in [Extended Foster Care](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-youth/extended-foster-care-program) * An assigned Licensing Division worker who can help with questions * Access to the [Foster Parent Reimbursement and Liability Plan](https://dcyf.wa.gov/publications-library?combine_1=&combine=reimbursement&field_program_topic_2_value=All&field_languages_available_value=All) for unexpected damages [Learn more about becoming a kinship caregiver](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/caring-for-relatives-child). ### DCYF’s kin-first culture DCYF believes that kinship caregivers are the best option when a child or youth cannot live with their parents. More than half of all children and youth experiencing foster care are cared for by a kinship caregiver. Children and youth who experience foster care are often removed from their homes, neighborhoods, communities, schools, pets, and friends. They also frequently lose connections with extended family and the traditions, language, and history maintained and passed on by these relationships. We know that when children are placed with kin, they are more likely to maintain these connections and culture and experience more stability (compared to children in foster care). Research shows that benefits of kinship care can include: * Less time spent in foster care * Higher placement stability * Lower chance of experiencing abuse by a caregiver * Greater likelihood of staying connected to their extended family and keeping ties to their cultures, backgrounds, and customs, which is critical to their well-being * Better behavioral and mental health outcomes These are all reasons why DCYF is committed to a "kin-first" culture that places young people experiencing foster care with relatives and close family friends first! ## Foster parenting The main goal of foster care is to provide safe, loving, temporary homes. As a foster parent, you take care of a child until they can be placed with kin, reunited with their parents, or reach permanency through guardianship or adoption. Foster parents help keep children connected to their families, schools, communities, and cultures. **Foster parents must be licensed before caring for children.**  The Department of Children, Youth, and Families (DCYF) is looking for foster parents who: * Know that foster care is temporary * Believe children and families can grow  * Value sibling relationships and keeping siblings together * Come from diverse racial and cultural backgrounds * Support LGBTQIA+ children and youth * Want to support the child or youth’s connections with their family, school, community, and culture * Want to care for children and youth with complex medical needs   **As a foster parent, you receive free training and monthly payments \[add link] that reimburse you for the care you provide.**  Learn more about becoming a foster parent. \[add link] {% Callout "note", "Note" %} Our priority is to reunite children and youth with their parents. When reunification is not possible, we look for kin who can take care of the child. Guardianship and adoption by foster parents are only options for children and youth who cannot return to their parents and do not have any kin available to care for them.  People who want to adopt Washington state foster children or youth must become fully licensed as foster parents. [add link] DCYF does not complete adoption-only home studies. Working with a private adoption agency may be a better fit for your family if you are not open to maintaining family connections or are not open to caring for a child with special needs. [Learn more about adoption](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/adoption). {% endCallout %} ### Therapeutic Foster Care (TFC) Therapeutic Foster Care (TFC) is a specialized form of foster care for young people with complex mental and behavioral health needs.  **Caregivers must be certified to provide TFC.** Getting certified requires extra training to make sure you’re prepared to support children and youth who need therapeutic services, as well as their families. **As a caregiver through TFC, you receive additional payments** and ongoing training. Professional Therapeutic Foster Care is another form of TFC that is currently in development. Learn more about TFC and how to get certified. \[add link] ## Voluntary placement agreement (VPA) Voluntary placement agreements (VPAs) are short-term caregiving arrangements that happen without a court order.  Instead, they happen when a child or youth’s parents voluntarily agree to have the child temporarily live with another caregiver. Most VPAs last up to 90 days. **You do not need a license to be a caregiver in a VPA.** If you are a caregiver in a VPA, you cannot become a licensed kinship caregiver. Because the child or youth is not in DCYF’s care, **you cannot get payments as a caregiver in a VPA.** You are not eligible for guardianship assistance program subsidies. [Learn more about voluntary placement agreements (VPAs)](https://dcyf.wa.gov/policies-and-procedures/4307-voluntary-placement-agreement). ## Respite care Respite is short-term care for children and youth in DCYF’s custody. It gives full-time caregivers a chance to take a break and avoid getting burned out. Respite care can happen in the child or youth’s full-time home or, in some cases, in the respite care provider’s home.  **You do not need a license to provide respite care, but you do need to be approved by the child or youth’s caseworker before caring for them.** You can provide respite care for children and youth you already know or for other children and youth who are in DCYF’s custody. **You can get a small daily payment for providing respite care.**  Learn more about becoming a respite care provider.
