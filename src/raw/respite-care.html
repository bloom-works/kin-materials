--- layout: layouts/rawAllCaregivers.njk title: Respite care templateEngineOverride: md,njk --- {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for caregivers of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on respite care. {% endCallout %} Respite care is a system that lets caregivers take temporary breaks from caregiving. The Department of Children, Youth, and Families (DCYF) can pay caregivers to provide respite care, while continuing monthly foster care payments to the primary caregiver as usual. Respite care gives you time to take care of yourself and attend to other responsibilities. It helps keep kids stable in your home and supports everyone’s health and well-being.  Respite care is available for all caregivers of children and youth who are in DCYF’s care. This includes children and youth who are in: **\[add links]** * Foster care * Kinship care * A voluntary placement agreements (VPA) * An in-home dependency or a trial return home   Respite care is one of several kinds of substitute care that you can use when you need a break. Learn more about all the options for substitute care, **\[add link]** including babysitting and licensed child care providers. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} If a child or youth you were caring for returns home and you are willing to provide respite for the family, let the caseworker know. This is a wonderful way to support families who are reunifying. Learn more about providing respite care. **\[add link]** {% endCallout %} This page includes information about: **\[add jump links]** * When to use respite care * How respite care works * How to swap respite care with another caregiver * How to request respite care ## **When to use respite care** Some reasons why you might use respite care include: * Work events * Medical appointments for yourself or family members * Vacations or trips * Training opportunities * Family or community events * Time for yourself * Time to spend one-on-one with a specific child or youth Anytime you need substitute care that will last for more than 72 hours, you have to use respite care. If you have multiple children or youth placed in your home, they can be in respite care together or separately. This lets you decide whether you want time for yourself or quality time with specific children or youth. If you are planning occasional care that will last for less than 72 hours and DCYF is not paying the caregiver, then it will often be considered [prudent parenting](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0078.pdf) rather than respite care. You are not required to tell the caseworker in these cases, but we encourage you to let them know ahead of time when you can. It’s always helpful for the caseworker to know where children and youth are and offer support when needed.  If you’re not sure whether respite makes sense in a specific situation, talk to the caseworker or your [Caregiver Supports](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/caregiver-supports-project/phase-two) team. They can help you figure out the best option to meet everyone’s needs. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} When you can, it’s great to include children and youth in your family’s events and vacations instead of using respite. If you want to do this, make sure that your plans follow the rules for traveling with children and youth **\[link to WAC page]** and do not interfere with the child or youth’s family visits. **\[link to WAC page]** The caseworker, guardian ad litem (GAL), or court-appointed special advocate (CASA) can connect you with resources for including children or youth in trips and activities. {% endCallout %} ## **How respite care works** Once you decide to use respite care, submit your request **\[add jump link]** as soon as possible. That way, the caseworker can make sure that children and youth are safe, that you are following all the necessary rules, and that the respite care provider can get paid. There are two basic models for respite care: * Another caregiver can come to your home to care for children or youth * Children or youth can stay with another caregiver Respite care can be provided by someone you already know or by a caregiver that DCYF finds for you. If you want someone you know to provide respite care, they may need to get a background check or get certified before providing care. Learn more about who can be a respite care provider **\[add link]** and how they can become a respite care provider. **\[add link]** If you ask DCYF to find a respite care provider, DCYF will do its best to find a caregiver to match your needs. However, it is not always possible to find a caregiver through DCYF. It is often easier to arrange respite care when a friend, family member, or other foster parent you know is available to be the respite caregiver. People who provide respite care can receive small payments from DCYF. You can also choose to pay them yourself. Learn more about payments for respite care providers. **\[add link]** ### **Respite care in your home** If you want to get respite care in your home, you can choose someone you trust (like a relative, a friend, or another foster parent) as the respite caregiver. Tell the caseworker you want this person to temporarily care for the child or youth.  If you are planning to have DCYF pay the respite caregiver,the person you choose needs to get approved through DCYF beforehand. They either need to complete the respite certification process **\[add link]** or get permission from the caseworker to care for your specific child or youth, which usually involves a background check and may include other steps. Child-specific permission is sometimes known as unlicensed respite. The respite care provider cannot get paid for providing respite if they live in your home.  If DCYF is **not** going to pay the respite caregiver,the caseworker still has to approve the caregiver. This usually involves a [background check](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/background-checks) and may include other steps. You can also ask DCYF, your Child Placing Agency (CPA), or your [Caregiver Supports](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/caregiver-supports-project/phase-two) team to find a certified respite provider to come to your home. There’s no guarantee that they will be able to find someone, so it’s helpful to make your request as soon as possible and have a backup plan. ### **Respite care outside your home** If you want a child or youth to stay outside your home during respite care, they can go to a licensed foster home or a licensed child care provider. This respite provider can either be one you find yourself or one DCYF finds for you. **If you are currently a kinship caregiver,** the child or youth can also go to the home of someone else who is their kin, such as another relative or family friend. These caregivers can get paid for providing respite. * If the respite caregiver is already a licensed kinship caregiver for other children or youth, they can ask their licensor to add the child or youth who will be in respite care to their license. * If the respite caregiver is not already a licensed caregiver, they will need to complete the certification process **\[add link]** to become a certified kin respite provider, as well as a [walkthrough of their home](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/roles-responsibilities/home-conditions) to check for safety hazards. This process is shorter than the full home study process. **\[add link]** ## **How to swap respite care with another caregiver** Licensed caregivers can agree to provide occasional care for each other’s children and youth. The two caregivers can arrange care according to whatever schedule works for them. Swaps can be a helpful way to set up care that meets both families’ needs and does not require much involvement from DCYF. **DCYF does not provide payments for caregivers in respite care swaps.** Caregivers are allowed to pay each other out of pocket if they choose. If you want to swap respite care with another caregiver, be sure to follow these rules: * Both caregivers have to meet the licensing requirements for the children or youth they will be caring for. For example, if you are only licensed to care for older children and teenagers, you cannot care for a baby in a respite care swap. * Both caregivers must stay within their licensed capacity. For example, if you are licensed to care for four children, you cannot be in charge of five children during a respite swap. * The respite arrangement has to follow the safety plans of all children and youth involved. * If one or both caregivers are licensed through a Child Placing Agency (CPA), they will have to follow any rules that the CPA has for providing respite. * Both caregivers have to get the plan to swap approved by the caseworkers and regional placement desks they work with. * Children and youth can only stay in homes that have been fully licensed through the home study process. **\[add link]** ## **How to request respite care** Respite care can happen at any time. You have to tell the caseworker whenever you plan to use respite care, even if DCYF is not paying the respite caregiver.  If you want DCYF to pay the respite caregiver, send your request for respite care as soon as you know you need it, ideally at least 14 days in advance. It’s especially important to send your request early if you want respite care during a busy time, such as school holidays or breaks.  There is no guarantee that DCYF will be able to find a caregiver, so it may be helpful to have someone you know prepared to provide respite if need be. Learn more about how they can become a respite care provider. **\[add link]** Whether you are requesting paid respite care or sharing a plan for unpaid respite, follow this process: * **Email all of these people:** * * The caseworker * The regional placement desk **\[add link]** * Your CPA (if you’re licensed by a CPA) * Your [Caregiver Supports](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/caregiver-supports-project/phase-two) placement support manager (if you have one) * **Include this information**: * * Your name and contact information * The dates you are planning to use respite care * Whether you are requesting respite to meet your own needs or the needs of a child or youth in your care (for example, if you are requesting respite for one child so that you can take another child to the doctor) * Any special needs, such as medical or behavioral needs * Any appointments for the child or youth that are scheduled during the dates you’re requesting * If you have someone lined up to provide respite care, include their name and contact information and note if they are a certified respite provider or certified kinship respite provider. * If you plan to swap respite with another caregiver, include any plans that you and the other caregiver have agreed on. * **Send reminders or confirmation emails** to the people you included in your first email, copying anyone you have lined up to provide respite. This can help with planning and communication between DCYF and respite caregivers. If your plan does not meet respite care requirements, the caseworker will let you know and work with you to make an updated plan.
