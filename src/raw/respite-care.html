--- layout: layouts/rawAllCaregivers.njk title: Respite care templateEngineOverride: md,njk --- {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for caregivers of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on respite care. {% endCallout %} Respite care is a system that lets caregivers take temporary breaks from caregiving. The Department of Children, Youth, and Families (DCYF) can pay caregivers to provide respite care, while continuing monthly foster care payments to the primary caregiver as usual. Respite care gives you time to take care of yourself and attend to other responsibilities. It helps keep kids stable in your home and supports everyone’s health and well-being.  **Respite care is available for all caregivers of children and youth who are in DCYF’s care.** This includes children and youth who are in \[add jump links]: * Foster care * Kinship care * A voluntary placement agreements (VPA) * An in-home dependency or a trial return home   **Anytime you need substitute care that will last for more than 72 hours or happen on a regular basis, you have to use respite care.** There are also other ways to take breaks from caregiving. Learn more about all the options for getting substitute care \[add link], including babysitting, licensed child care providers, and prudent parenting. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} If a child or youth you were caring for returns home and you are willing to provide respite for the family, let the caseworker know. This is a wonderful way to support families who are reunifying. Learn more about providing respite care \[add link]. {% endCallout %} This page includes information about \[add jump links]: * When to use respite care * Who can provide respite care * How respite care works {% Callout "note", "Note" %} If you’re part of a Mockingbird Constellation, you may have different options for respite care. Learn more about Mockingbird Constellations \[add link]. {% endCallout %} ## When to use respite care Anytime you need substitute care that will last for more than 72 hours or happen on a regular basis, you have to use respite care. Some reasons why you might use respite care include: * Work events * Medical appointments for yourself or family members * Vacations or trips * Training opportunities * Family or community events * Time for yourself * Time to spend one-on-one with a specific child or youth If you are planning occasional care that will last for less than 72 hours and DCYF is not paying the caregiver, then it will often be considered [prudent parenting](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0078.pdf) rather than respite care. You are not required to tell the caseworker in these cases, but we encourage you to let them know ahead of time when you can. It’s always helpful for the caseworker to know where children and youth are and offer support when needed.  If you have multiple children or youth placed in your home, they can be in respite care together or separately. This lets you decide whether you want time for yourself or quality time with specific children or youth. If you are licensed through a Child Placing Agency (CPA), they may have additional guidelines and processes for getting respite care. The rules and processes for using respite care can be confusing. If you’re not sure whether respite makes sense in a specific situation, talk to the caseworker, your CPA, or your [Caregiver Supports](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/caregiver-supports-project/phase-two) team. They can help you figure out the best option to meet everyone’s needs. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} When you can, it’s great to include children and youth in your family’s events and vacations instead of using respite. If you want to do this, make sure that your plans follow the rules for traveling with children and youth \[add link] and do not interfere with the child or youth’s family time visits \[add link]. The caseworker, guardian ad litem (GAL), or court-appointed special advocate (CASA) can connect you with resources for including children or youth in trips and activities. If you’re not sure whether you can include a child or youth in your plans, talk to the caseworker. {% endCallout %} ## Who can provide respite care Respite care can be provided by someone you already know or by a caregiver that DCYF finds for you. There are many different ways to become a respite care provider, and some people choose to become certified respite care providers. Learn more about who can be a respite care provider and how certification works \[add link]. **You do not have to use a certified respite care provider.** Anyone you know and trust (like a friend, relative, or other foster parent) can get a background check and permission to provide respite for specific children or youth at your home. This kind of respite care is called child-specific or unlicensed respite. It is usually easier to arrange respite care when someone you know is available to be the respite caregiver. If you ask DCYF to find a respite care provider, DCYF will do its best to find a caregiver to match your needs. However, it is not always possible to find a caregiver through DCYF.  People who provide respite care can receive small payments from DCYF. **Caregivers who are not certified can still get these payments.** Unlike foster care payments, respite care payments are taxable income. Learn more about payments for respite care providers \[add link]. ## How respite care works Once you decide to use respite care, submit your request \[add link] as soon as possible. That way, the caseworker can make sure that children and youth will be safe during respite, that you are following all the necessary rules, and that the respite care provider can get paid. There are two basic models for respite care: * Another caregiver can come to your home to care for children or youth * Children or youth can stay with another caregiver In both models, you can either find a respite provider yourself or ask DCYF, your CPA, or your [Caregiver Supports](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/caregiver-supports-project/phase-two) team to find one. There’s no guarantee that they will be able to find someone, so it’s helpful to make your request as soon as possible and have a backup plan. ### Respite care in your home Any respite care provider can provide respite care in your home. The caseworker will make sure that the provider is approved and set up to get payments. Even if you do not plan on having DCYF pay the provider,the caseworker still has to approve them. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} The respite care provider cannot get paid for providing respite if they live in your home. {% endCallout %} ### Respite care outside your home If you want the child or youth to stay outside your home during respite care, they can go to a **licensed foster home** or a **licensed child care provider.**  The child or youth may also be able to stay at the home of **someone who is their kin, such as a relative or family friend.** This applies to children and youth who are in foster care, as well as those who are in kinship care.  * If the respite provider is already a licensed kinship caregiver for other children or youth, they can ask their licensor to add the child or youth who will be in respite care to their license. * If not, they will need to complete a certification process \[add link] to become a certified kin respite provider, as well as a [walkthrough of their home](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/roles-responsibilities/home-conditions) to check for safety hazards. This process is shorter than the full home study process \[add link]. * Like other respite providers, these caregivers can get paid for providing respite.
