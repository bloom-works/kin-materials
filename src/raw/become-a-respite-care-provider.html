--- layout: layouts/rawAllCaregivers.njk title: Become a respite care provider templateEngineOverride: md,njk --- Respite care providers support children, youth, and their caregivers by taking care of children and youth for short periods of time.  Respite care gives full-time caregivers time and support to take care of themselves and attend to other necessary responsibilities. It’s an important way to help caregivers avoid burnout and keep children and youth in stable living situations. ## Who can be a respite care provider Anyone over the age of 18 can apply to become a certified respite care or kinship respite provider. Here are a few common scenarios:   * **You’re a licensed foster parent** and you want to keep supporting a child or youth who used to be placed with you. * **You know a child, youth, or family** who is eligible for respite care [**add link**] and you want to support them.  * **You’re interested in becoming a foster parent** or supporting children and youth who are in DCYF’s care. Learn more about certification requirements [**add link**] and whether they apply to you. You can get a small payment for providing respite care. Learn more about payments for respite care providers. [**add link**] ## How to become a respite care provider There are several pathways to providing respite care [**add jump links**]: * Licensed foster parenting * Certified respite care * Certified kin respite * Unlicensed respite care  This page explains these options and what steps to take for each one. All respite care providers can receive small payments. Learn more about payments for respite care providers. [**add link**] {% Callout "note", "Note" %} Before you provide respite care, make sure you are familiar with the process for requesting respite care [**add link**] and that the child or youth’s full-time caregiver follows the steps in the process. If they do not, you may not be able to get paid. {% endCallout %} ### Licensed foster parenting If you are a licensed foster parent, you are already allowed to provide respite care and do not need to get certified. Your licensor can help you make sure you have everything you need to provide respite care. You can provide respite care in your licensed foster home, in another licensed foster home, or in the home where the child or youth lives.  You have to follow these rules when providing respite: * You have to meet the licensing requirements for the children or youth you will be caring for. For example, if you are only licensed to care for older children and teenagers, you cannot care for a baby during respite. * You must stay within your licensed capacity [**add link**] while providing respite care. For example, if you are licensed to care for four children, you cannot be in charge of five children during respite.  * The respite arrangement has to follow the safety plans of all children and youth involved.  * If you are licensed through a Child Placing Agency (CPA), you have to follow any rules that the CPA has for providing respite. Learn more about becoming a licensed foster parent. [**add link**] ### Certified respite care Becoming a certified respite care provider lets you provide respite care to children and youth who are in DCYF’s custody and who you do not have an existing relationship with. This path will likely make sense for you if you’re interested in foster parenting but are not ready to go through the full foster parent licensing process. [**add link**] To get certified,  you must complete these steps: * Get in touch with the Department of Children, Youth, and Families (DCYF), a Child Placing Agency (CPA), or a Tribal CPA. * * To contact DCYF, fill out the [Foster Parent Inquiry form](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/questionnaire) or call 1-888-KIDS-414.  * To contact a CPA, refer to the [list of CPA contacts](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/child-placing-agencies/cpa-contacts). * Complete an online application. * Complete additional certification steps, which may include background checks and interviews.  As a certified respite care provider, you can only provide respite care in a fully licensed foster home. You cannot provide respite care in your own home unless you become a fully licensed foster parent. ### Certified kin respite If you know a child, youth, or family who needs a respite care provider, you can provide respite for them through certified kin respite. This can be a great way to support the family without becoming a licensed caregiver. If you are already a [licensed kinship caregiver](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/overview), you do not need to get certified. Follow the steps for licensed kinship caregivers [**add jump link to next section**] instead. To get certified, you must complete these steps: * Contact the child or youth’s DCYF or Tribal caseworker to start the process. * Complete an online application.  * Complete additional certification steps, which may include background checks and interviews.  Certified kin respite providers can only provide respite care when they have an existing relationship with the child, youth, or family. The goal of certified kin respite is to keep children and youth connected with their families and communities. As a certified kin respite provider, you may be able to provide respite care in your own home. If you are interested in doing this, request permission during your certification process. You may have to complete some additional steps. #### Steps for licensed kinship caregivers If you are a [licensed kinship caregiver](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/overview), you can provide respite care for other children and youth who you have existing relationships with. You do not need to go through an additional certification process. Instead, you can ask your licensing worker to add the additional child or youth to your license.  For example, let’s say you are a licensed kinship caregiver for your niece and another relative cares for your niece’s brother. You can tell your licensing worker you want to temporarily care for your niece’s brother sometimes. If the change is approved, you can get respite payments when you care for the brother. These respite payments will be in addition to the monthly reimbursements you already get for caring for your niece. If you want to provide respite care for children and youth who you do **not** have existing relationships with, talk to your licensor about updating your license to a foster care license rather than a kinship license. This will allow you to provide respite care for other children and youth. ### Child-specific respite You can get permission to provide respite care for specific children or youth if you have an existing relationship with them or their full-time caregiver. Child-specific respite is sometimes called unlicensed respite. For example, if you have a friend who is a licensed foster parent, you can get permission to provide respite care for the specific children or youth your friend cares for. You do not need to get certified and can still receive payments for providing respite care. In order for you to provide child-specific respite, the child or youth’s caseworker has to run a background check and approve you to provide respite. The caseworker grants approval on a case-by-case basis. The child or youth’s full-time caregiver can talk to the caseworker to start this process.
