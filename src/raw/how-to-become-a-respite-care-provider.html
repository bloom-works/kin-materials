--- layout: layouts/rawAllCaregivers.njk title: How to become a respite care provider templateEngineOverride: md,njk --- There are several pathways to providing respite care: * Licensed foster parenting [**add jump links**] * Certified respite care (test test test) * Certified kin respite * Unlicensed respite care  This page explains these options and what steps to take for each one. All respite care providers can receive small payments. Learn more about [payments for respite care providers](https://dcyf-kin-materials.netlify.app/raw/payments-for-respite-care-providers). {% Callout "note", "Note" %} Before you provide respite care, make sure you are familiar with the process for requesting respite care [**link to full respite page**] and that the child or youth’s full-time caregiver follows the steps in the process. {% endCallout %} ## Licensed foster parenting If you are a licensed foster parent,you are already allowed to provide respite care and do not need to get certified. Your licensor can help you make sure you have everything you need to provide respite care. You can provide respite care in your licensed foster home, in another licensed foster home, or in the home where the child or youth lives.  You have to follow these rules when providing respite: * You have to meet the licensing requirements for the children or youth you will be caring for. For example, if you are only licensed to care for older children and teenagers, you cannot care for a baby in a respite care swap. * You must stay within your licensed capacity [**link to capacity page**] while providing respite care. For example, if you are licensed to care for four children, you cannot be in charge of five children during respite.  * The respite arrangement has to follow the safety plans of all children and youth involved.  * If you are licensed through a Child Placing Agency (CPA), you have to follow any rules that the CPA has for providing respite. Learn more about [becoming a licensed foster parent](https://dcyf-kin-materials.netlify.app/raw/get-a-license-to-become-a-foster-parent/). ## Certified respite care Becoming a certified respite care provider lets you provide respite care to children and youth who are in DCYF’s custody and who you do not have an existing relationship with. This path will likely make sense for you if you’re interested in foster parenting but are not ready to go through the full [foster parent licensing process](https://dcyf-kin-materials.netlify.app/raw/get-a-license-to-become-a-foster-parent/). To get certified, you must complete these steps: * Get in touch with the Department of Children, Youth, and Families (DCYF), a Child Placing Agency (CPA), or a Tribal CPA. * * To contact DCYF, fill out the [Foster Parent Inquiry form](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/questionnaire) or call 1-888-KIDS-414.  * To contact a CPA, refer to the [list of CPA contacts](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/child-placing-agencies/cpa-contacts). * Complete an online application. * Complete additional certification steps, which may include background checks and interviews.  As a certified respite care provider, you can only provide respite care in a fully licensed foster home. You cannot provide respite care in your own home unless you become a fully licensed foster parent. ## Certified kin respite If you know a child, youth, or family who needs a respite care provider, you can provide respite for them through certified kin respite. This can be a great way to support the family without becoming a licensed caregiver. If you are already a [licensed kinship caregiver](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/overview), you do not need to get certified. Follow the steps for licensed kinship caregivers \[jump link to next section] instead. To get certified,  you must complete these steps: * Contact the child or youth’s DCYF or Tribal caseworker to start the process. * Complete an online application.  * Complete additional certification steps, which may include background checks and interviews.  Certified kin respite providers can only provide respite care when they have an existing relationship with the child, youth, or family. The goal of certified kin respite is to keep children and youth connected with their families and communities. As a certified kin respite provider, you may be able to provide respite care in your own home. If you are interested in doing this, request permission during your certification process. You may have to complete some additional steps. ### Steps for licensed kinship caregivers If you are a [licensed kinship caregiver](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/overview), you can provide respite care for other children and youth who you have existing relationships with. You do not need to go through an additional certification process. Instead, you can ask your licensing worker to add the additional child or youth to your license.  For example, let’s say you are a licensed kinship caregiver for your niece and another relative cares for your niece’s brother. You can tell your licensing worker you want to temporarily care for your niece’s brother sometimes. If the change is approved, you can get respite payments when you care for the brother. These respite payments will be in addition to the monthly reimbursements you already get for caring for your niece. If you want to provide respite care for children and youth who you do **not** have existing relationships with, talk to your licensor about updating your license to a foster care license rather than a kinship license. This will allow you to provide respite care for other children and youth. ## Unlicensed respite Unlicensed respite gives you permission to provide respite care for specific children or youth. You must have an existing relationship with the child or youth, or with their full-time caregiver. For example, if you have a friend who is a licensed foster parent, you may be able to provide unlicensed respite care for the children or youth your friend cares for. You do not need to get certified and can still receive payments for providing respite care. In order for you to provide unlicensed respite, the child or youth’s caseworker has to run a background check and approve you to provide respite. The caseworker grants approval on a case-by-case basis. The child or youth’s full-time caregiver can talk to the caseworker to start this process. You can only provide unlicensed respite for the specific children or youth you have been approved to care for.
