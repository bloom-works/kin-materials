--- layout: layouts/rawAllCaregivers.njk title: Guardianship templateEngineOverride: md,njk --- DCYF’s primary goal is for children and youth to return home to their parents. When that’s not possible, we explore guardianship as an option to give a child or youth a safe, stable, and permanent home.  Keep reading to learn more about what it means to be someone’s guardian.  On this page    * What guardianship means * What happens when you agree to become a guardian * * Your responsibilities as a guardian * Public benefits * Visits with family * Financial support   * The process of becoming a guardian Note: This page is about Title 13 guardianships only. These guardianships are completed through DCYF.  The information on this page is for caregivers of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on permanency options.  ## **What guardianship means** Guardianship is a legal arrangement where parents no longer make decisions for their child. Instead, the court appoints another adult to take legal responsibility for caring for the child or youth, ensuring their safety, and making decisions for them until they turn 18.  The parents are still the child or youth’s parents on paper. (For example, the child or youth keeps the right to inherit from the parents.) Guardianship can provide the child or youth with a safe, stable living situation, while allowing them to maintain a relationship with their parents.   Sometimes, legally free youth (youth with parents whose rights have been terminated in court) prefer to have guardians instead of being adopted. ## **What happens when you become a guardian** When the court appoints you to be a child or youth’s guardian, the child or youth is no longer in foster care. Their DCYF case will close, and DCYF will no longer be responsible for their care. For example, the child or youth will no longer have a caseworker who visitsDC monthly. DCYF will no longer facilitate visits between the child or youth, and their parents.  ### Your responsibilities as a guardian  As a guardian, you’re responsible for most or all aspects of caring for the child or youth until they turn 18. That means you’re responsible for:  * Providing them with food, clothing, housing, and education * Making decisions about their social and school activities * Keeping them safe, and setting healthy boundaries for them  * Protecting the child or youth’s financial assets, and teaching them how to manage their finances * Supporting the child or youth’s visits with their parents, if the court order requires visits A judge will issue a guardianship order that describes your responsibilities, and any rights and responsibilities the parents still have.  ### Visits with family  Guardianship gives a child or youth a safe, permanent home with someone they know and trust, without ending the parents’ rights. It is meant to last, while recognizing that children and youth may still have important relationships from their past. Visits can help honor connections between children and youth, and their family. As a guardian, you must follow the visitation plan in the judge’s guardianship order. Depending on the order, you may have to bring the child or youth to their parents, or supervise visits.  During the process of becoming a guardian, you can share your thoughts about visits in court. If visits have already been happening, you may have a sense of what works best for you and the child or youth. It helps to come to court with an idea of what feels realistic.  The judge will issue a court order that usually includes a visitation plan. Once the judge issues the order, you must follow it. Any changes to the visitation plan have to be approved in court. Agreeing to a clear minimum plan upfront can help prevent confusion later. ### Financial support  **If you’re a licensed kinship caregiver or foster parent** **and you later become a guardian for a child or youth**, you may become eligible for monthly financial support as a guardian through the Guardianship Assistance Program (GAP). You must have received at least six consecutive months of foster care payments to qualify.  [Learn about the full eligibility requirements for the Guardianship Assistance Program](https://dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0128.pdf).  **If you’re an unlicensed kinship caregiver who is interested in becoming the child or youth’s guardian and you want to get financial support through GAP in the future**, you’ll first need to [go through DCYF’s process to become a licensed kinship caregiver for the child or youth](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/placement-requirements).  If you do not get licensed, you will not be eligible for financial support through GAP. But you can still apply for financial support through Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF). [Learn more about about TANF](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/roles-responsibilities/payments/tanf).  **Note:** Guardians for children and youth under the care of Tribal Courts of federally recognized Tribes may be able to get financial support through the Tribal Guardianship Assistance Program (TGAP). You must meet the same eligibility requirements as GAP.  ### Public benefits  If you apply for public benefits for the child or youth, the state benefit program may need to collect child support from the child or youth’s parents, unless you request an exception (called a good cause waiver). **Note:** If you receive Guardianship Assistance Program payments, the child or youth is not eligible for TANF. They may still be eligible for other state benefit programs like SNAP. ## **The process of becoming a guardian** Becoming a guardian looks different for every caregiver. It depends on the child or youth, their family’s situation, and conversations between the family, you, and the caseworker. When a child or youth is in DCYF’s care, we hold Youth and Family Meetings (YFMs) to talk about long‑term plans. Early on, parents are often working with DCYF to access services and work toward reunification. Over time, if reunification does not seem likely, we may discuss guardianship as a possible permanent option. These conversations usually happen gradually. They include you, the youth, family members, and the caseworker. DCYF will talk through what guardianship would mean for the child or youth, and for your family. The law requires DCYF and the court to consider whether guardianship can provide long‑term stability and safety for children and youth. They only consider terminating parental rights when reunification is no longer possible and guardianship is not a good fit. As a caregiver, your voice matters. Your experiences, observations, and willingness to provide a permanent home are important parts of the process. We’ll ask for your input as decisions move forward. After lots of conversations about whether guardianship is in the child or youth’s best interest, we may recommend it to the court. We’ll file the required paperwork. If you’re willing to be the guardian, we’ll ask you to complete a legal document called a Declaration of Guardianship. You’ll discuss plans for visitation between the child or youth, and their parents, with the court. If you’re interested in getting financial help through the Guardianship Assistance Program (GAP), you must complete an application for the program, and an agreement before the court finalizes the guardianship. Depending on your situation, there may be other paperwork you’ll need to complete.  Ultimately, the court—not DCYF—makes the final decision about which permanency plan is in the child or youth’s best interests. ## **After the youth turns 18** If you get a Guardianship Assistance Program subsidy for a youth before they turn 18, you may be eligible to keep getting subsidies through the Extended Guardianship Assistance Program (EGAP) until the youth turns 21.  The goal of EGAP is to help you provide a stable environment for the youth as they transition to adulthood.  You may be eligible for EGAP if one of these is true:  * The youth is enrolled in high school or a high school equivalency program.  * The youth is enrolled in, has applied for, or can show they plan to enroll soon in  post-secondary education or a vocational program.  * The youth works part-time, or is in a program to helps them get a job (for example, training for job readiness, or new skills) * The youth works 80 hours or more a month.  * The youth has a documented medical condition that prevents them from doing any of these activities. You and the youth must apply for EGAP together. [Learn more about EGAP](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0121.pdf).  Note: Guardians for youth under the care of Tribal Courts of federally recognized Tribes may be able to get financial support through the Extended Tribal Guardianship Assistance Program (ETGAP) after the youth turns 18. You must meet the same eligibility requirements as EGAP.
