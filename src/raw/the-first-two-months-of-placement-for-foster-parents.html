If you are a kinship caregiver, [learn more about the first two months of placement for kinship caregivers](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/first-two-months-placement). You may be a kinship caregiver for a child or youth if you’re their relative or family friend. {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for foster parents of children and youth in DCYF’s custody only.  Individual Tribes have different requirements for foster parents of children and youth in the custody of a Tribe. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on the first two months of placement. {% endCallout %} {% Callout "related", "On this page" %} On this page:  * What to expect in the first 10 days of placement  * What you need to do  * * Education * Health care  * Record keeping * Other things you need to do * How to work with the caseworker  * Other people who will contact you {% endCallout %} ## What to expect in the first 10 days of placement In the first few days of placement, a lot happens. Many people will contact you or visit your home. You may have to attend meetings, hearings, or appointments for a child or youth. It’s normal to feel overwhelmed or confused.  Around the first week of placement, you’ll hear from your CaRES mentor. We **strongly** encourage you to connect with your CaRES mentor for advice about how to navigate the caregiver experience, and advocate for yourself. Here’s what to expect in the first 10 days of placement: * **A caseworker will visit you and the child** **or youth** at home during their first week with you. This is called a Health and Safety Visit. The caseworker will check on how things are going. They’ll also spend some time alone with the child or youth to hear how they’re doing. * **The child or youth will have family time visits** with their parents and siblings. Follow the court and the caseworker's instructions about family time. We may ask you to transport the child or youth, and to supervise visits. You’re welcome to do it, but you do not have to. Learn more about family time visits (**add link**). * **There will be a Family Team Decision-Making meeting within the first three days, if DCYF did not hold one earlier**. This meeting is an opportunity to learn more about why the child or youth is in foster care. You should receive an invitation to this meeting, and we strongly encourage you to attend. There may be parts of the meeting when we may need you to step out in order to protect the parents’ privacy. And you may get invitations to other meetings (also called shared planning meetings), depending on the child or youth's situation. [Learn more about the Family Team Decision-Making meeting and other shared planning meetings.](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0070.pdf) * **Many people will contact or visit you about the child or youth’s care.** We understand this can be overwhelming. Learn about other people who will contact you, and what their roles are (**add link**).   * **Sometimes, the caseworker may change.** Learn more about what to do when the caseworker changes (**add link**).  If Child Protective Services recently removed the child or youth from their parents, and this is child or youth’s first placement outside of their home, these things will also happen:  * **There will be a court "shelter care hearing" within the first three days**. [Read more about what happens at a shelter care hearing](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0065.pdf). * **You’ll get an Apple Health Core Connections interim voucher** that allows the child or youth to get medical care before their medical insurance coverage card arrives. The state automatically enrolls the child or youth in Apple Health Core Connections (AHCC), a specialized Medicaid managed care plan in Washington state. You can also ask the caseworker to look up the child or youth's Provider One or AHCC identification number in DCYF’s system. [Read the Apple Health Foster Care Member Handbook for more information about the child or youth’s medical coverage](https://www.coordinatedcarehealth.com/members/foster-care/member-resources/member-handbooks-and-forms.html). * **The state will mail you the child or youth’s medical insurance coverage card.** [Learn more about the timeline for meetings and court hearings in the first 2 months and beyond](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/sites/default/files/pubs/CWP_0065.pdf). {% Callout "note", "Note" %} It’s helpful to put all the paperwork you receive in one folder or binder. Whenever you go to a meeting or meet with a caseworker, bring it with you and add to it. This way, you have what you need when speaking with different professionals. {% endCallout %} ## What you need to do There are a few things you'll need to do after a child or youth is placed in your home. ### Education * **Keep the child or youth enrolled in and attending the same school.** The school district is responsible for arranging transportation to and from the school. If you believe the child or youth should move to a new school, please talk with the caseworker. The child or youth can only change schools if you go through a “best interest determination” meeting.  * **If a new school placement is approved, enroll the child or youth in a new school within three days.** The school district or districts must coordinate to ensure the child or youth’s records and school credits transfer. The school district is responsible for arranging transportation to and from school. * **Request copies of the child or youth’s Individualized Education Program (IEP), Individual Family Service Plan (IFSP), or 504 Plan, if they have one.** If the child or youth is changing schools, these records will be at the previous school. * If the student has outstanding school fees or fines, send the bills to the caseworker to pay them.  If you need help, contact the caseworker or ask to speak with the school district’s foster care liaison.  [Find the school district’s foster care liaison](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/student-success/access-opportunity-education/foster-care/foster-care-liaison-dcyf-and-state-wide-contacts). Learn more about your roles and responsibilities related to the child or youth’s education [**add link**].  ### Health care * **Schedule a dental exam if the child or youth has one or more teeth.** Within the first 30 calendar days of placement, you must contact a dentist and make an appointment for an initial dental examination. The exam must occur within the first 60 days of their placement. [Find dentists near you who accept the child or youth’s insurance](https://findaprovider.coordinatedcarehealth.com/location). * **Schedule and take the child or youth to a medical appointment:** * If the child or youth has an urgent medical need, take them to urgent care or an emergency room immediately. * If the child or youth has a medical need that is not urgent (like a chronic health issue, or other health concern), take them to an initial health screen within five days of their placement. And take them to [an EPSDT assessment or well-child exam](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/health-for-youth/epsdt) within 30 days of their placement. * If the child or youth does not have any medical needs, take them to [an EPSDT assessment or well-child exam](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/health-for-youth/epsdt) within 30 days of their placement. [Find medical providers near you who accept the child or youth’s insurance](https://findaprovider.coordinatedcarehealth.com/location). * **Make sure you have the child or youth’s medications**. When the child or youth arrives at your home, the caseworker is responsible for giving you the medications they take. Bring the medications to the child or youth’s medical appointments. Use the interim voucher for medical care to get refills. You can use the same pharmacy, or use a different pharmacy.  * **Talk with the caseworker first if you think the child or youth needs changes to their medications**. The caseworker needs to get permission before any changes to medications.  You’ll need to keep taking children and youth to regular medical and dental appointments after the first two months. Learn more about your roles and responsibilities related to health and medicine for foster children and youth (**add link**). ### Record keeping * **Sign up for DCYF's online portal for managing foster care payments, and submit your request for your first monthly payment**. Learn more about foster care payments [**add link**].  * **Keep records that you receive for the child or youth, and maintain them** (also called a well-being file). Add this information to their file, if it’s not already there:  * The child or youth’s name and birth date * The names and phone numbers of the caseworker, guardian ad litem, child advocate, and attorney * Medical information, immunization records, and medication records  * Written consent for providing medical care and emergency surgery, as authorized by a court order * A list of the child or youth’s personal belongings. Keep this list updated with any items they get while they live with you.   * The names, addresses, and phone numbers of emergency contacts * The names, addresses, and phone numbers for people who are allowed to have unsupervised access to the child or youth  * Any specific cultural, social, and emotional needs that the child or youth has * **Keep track of transportation expenses** on the [Caregiver Monthly Transportation Reimbursement form](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/forms?field_number_value=07-090&title=). And turn in complete forms to the caseworker to get reimbursed. ### Other things you need to do  * **Work with DCYF to arrange child care if you plan to use a child care center.** Talk to the caseworker to get a referral for a child care center that accepts DCYF payments. You cannot arrange or pay for child care on your own. This is to make sure that when the child is reunified with their family, the state can continue to pay for them to receive the same care. [Connect with Child Care Aware of Washington for help finding child care near you](https://childcareawarewa.org/).  * **Tell the caseworker about any unplanned parent-child contact,** such as phone calls or unexpected visits. * **Share any concerns about the child or youth with the caseworker.** Examples include abuse, neglect, medical, behavioral, developmental, or educational issues. If the child or youth tells you something that worries you, do not ask follow-up questions yourself. Instead, listen carefully, let the child or youth know that you heard them, and report what they told you to the caseworker. * **Tell the caseworker the dates, times, and purposes of any appointments** that you set up for the child or youth. ## How to work with the caseworker If you’re licensed through DCYF (instead of a CPA), the caseworker is your main point of contact. The caseworker’s job is to manage the whole case, including the needs of the child or youth’s parents and family. They do this by: * Looking after children and youth’s safety and well-being * Helping families reunify, or finding a stable, permanent living situation for children and youth * Working with you, the caregiver, to meet the needs of the child or youth and their family Caseworkers work with families to create a case plan, and report progress and challenges to the court. If you or the child or youth needs support or services, let the caseworker know. You must also report certain health and safety incidents to the caseworker. Learn which incidents you need to report (**add link**). The caseworker will visit you and the child or youth at least once a month. This will continue as long as the child or youth is in foster care. Some visits will be scheduled and others will be unannounced. Learn more about how to work with the caseworker (**add link**). ### Communicating with the caseworker Save the name, phone number, and email address for the caseworker and their supervisor. This information is in your placement paperwork. You may get a lot of phone calls from them, and it’s important to answer or call back as soon as you can. Tell the caseworker when and how you want them to reach you (for example, by phone, text, or email). Ask the caseworker what works best for them, too. If you do not know the caseworker or their supervisor’s phone number, [contact DCYF](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/contact-us). If you were certified by a CPA, contact the case manager at the CPA.  ### Caseworker changes Sometimes, we need to transfer a child or youth’s case to a new caseworker. If that happens, the previous caseworker will try to let you know. If you think we transferred the child or youth’s case, first try to contact the previous case worker, and ask them for the name and contact information for the new caseworker.  If you cannot reach the previous caseworker, contact their supervisor or [contact DCYF](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/contact-us). If you were certified by a CPA, contact the case manager at the CPA. ## Other people who will contact you Different professionals will contact you about the child or youth. If you have any questions about who these individuals are and what information you can discuss with them, please ask the caseworker or their supervisor. [Learn more about who else you can contact for support](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/publications-library/LIC_0081). Here are some of the professionals you might work with: * **Alliance CaRES:** Around the first week, you will hear from your CaRES mentor. Your mentor will answer your questions and support you as you care for a child or youth. The CaRES team can also provide referrals to other resources, including training and support groups. You can reach them at [alliancecares@uw.edu](mailto:alliancecares@uw.edu) or 206-221-4913. * **Someone from DCYF’s Child Health and Education Tracking (CHET) program:** A worker will contact you within 30 days of placement to help assess the child or youth’s medical needs.  * If the CHET report identifies any medical needs for the child or youth, call Apple Health Core Connections at 1-844-354-9876 and ask for help with coordinating health care.  * For help with the child or youth’s education, contact your [local school district's foster care liaison](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/student-success/access-opportunity-education/foster-care/foster-care-liaison-dcyf-and-state-wide-contacts).  * **Ongoing Mental Health (OMH) screener:** If the child or youth is 3 years old through 17 years old, a worker will contact you as part of the follow-up to the CHET assessment. They will check what emotional and behavioral health needs the child or youth has. You can request a referral for an OMH screener at any time. * **Child Advocates, Guardians ad Litem (GAL), and Court Appointed Special Advocates (CASA):** Their role is to advocate for children in court. The advocate may visit your home. * **The child or youth’s attorney:** Children and youth 12 and older may have an attorney. Children and youth whose parents’ rights have been terminated will have an attorney. The attorney may visit your home. {% Callout "related", "Related content" %} * Payments and financial support * Permanency planning * Healthcare for youth * Community and group support  * Legal support {% endCallout %}
