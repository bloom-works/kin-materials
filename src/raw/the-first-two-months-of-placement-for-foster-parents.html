--- layout: layouts/raw.njk title: The first two months of placement for foster parents templateEngineOverride: md,njk parent_page: Kinship caregiver rules parent_page_link: rules.html --- This page describes what happens and what you'll need to do when a foster child or youth is first placed with you. If you are a kinship caregiver, [learn more about the first two months of placement for kinship caregivers](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/foster-parenting/kinship-caregivers/becoming-kinship-caregiver/first-two-months-placement). {% Callout "note", "Note" %} This information is for foster parents of children and youth in DCYF’s care only. If a Tribe has placed a child or youth with you, contact the Tribe, or the Tribal caseworker, for more information on the first two months of placement. {% endCallout %} ## What to expect in the first 10 days of placement In the first few days of placement, there’s a lot that happens. It’s normal to feel overwhelmed or confused.  Here’s what to expect in the first 10 days of placement: * **A caseworker will visit you and the child** **or youth** at home during their first week with you. This is called a Health and Safety Visit. The caseworker will check on how things are going. They’ll also spend some time alone with the child or youth to hear how they’re doing. * **The child or youth will have family time visits** with their parents and siblings. It’s your responsibility to support the child or youth's connection to their parents and siblings. Follow the court and the caseworker's instructions about family time. Learn more about family time visits. * **There will be a meeting within the first three days, if there was no  Family Team Decision-Making (FTDM) meeting before the child or youth came into your care**. This meeting is an opportunity to learn more about why the child or youth is in foster care. Their parents may have to agree for you to attend this meeting. We strongly encourage you to attend the meeting if possible. You may get invitations to other meetings about caring for the child or youth (also called shared planning meetings) depending on their situation.  If Child Protective Services recently removed the child or youth from their parents, and this is child or youth’s first placement outside of their home, these things will also happen:  * **There will be a court "shelter care hearing" within the first three days**. Read more about [what happens at a shelter care hearing](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/publications-library?combine_1=CWP_0065&combine=&field_program_topic_value=All&field_languages_available_value=All). * **You’ll get an interim voucher** that allows you to access medical care for the child or youth while you wait for their medical insurance coverage card to arrive. You can also ask the caseworker to look up the child or youth's Provider One or Apple Health Core Connections identification number in DCYF’s system. * **The state will mail you the child or youth’s medical insurance coverage card.** ## What you need to do There are a few things you'll need to do after a child or youth is placed in your home. ### Education * **Keep the child or youth enrolled in and attending the same school.** If you believe the child or youth should move to a new school, please talk with the caseworker. The child or youth can only change schools if you go through a “best interest determination” meeting.  * **If a new school placement is approved, enroll the child or youth in a new school within three days.**  * **Request copies of the child or youth’s Individualized Education Program (IEP), Individual Family Service Plan (IFSP), or 504 Plan, if they have one.** * If the student has outstanding school fees or fines, send the bills to the caseworker to pay them.  If you need help, contact the caseworker or ask to speak with the [school district’s foster care liaison](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/student-success/accessopportunity-education/foster-care/foster-careliaisonsdcyf-contactsschool-contacts).  Learn more about your roles and responsibilities related to the child or youth’s education \[add link].  ### Health care * **Schedule a dental exam if the child or youth has one or more teeth.** Within the first 30 calendar days of placement, you must contact a dentist and make an appointment for an initial dental examination. The exam must occur within the first 60 days of their placement. * **Schedule and take the child or youth to a medical appointment:** * If the child or youth has an urgent medical need, take them to urgent care or an emergency room immediately. * If the child or youth has a medical need that is not urgent (like a chronic health issue, or other health concern), take them to an initial health screen within five days of their placement. And take them to [an EPSDT assessment or well-child exam](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/health-for-youth/epsdt) within 30 days of their placement. * If the child or youth does not have any medical needs, take them to [an EPSDT assessment or well-child exam](https://dcyf.wa.gov/services/health-for-youth/epsdt) within 30 days of their placement. * **Make sure you have the child or youth’s medications**. When the child or youth arrives at your home, the caseworker is responsible for giving you the medications they take. Bring the medications to the child or youth’s medical appointments. Use the interim voucher for medical care to get refills. You can use the same pharmacy, or use a different pharmacy.  * **Talk with the caseworker first if you think the child or youth needs changes to their medications**. The caseworker needs to get permission before any changes to medications.  ### Record keeping * **Keep records that you receive for the child or youth, and maintain them** (also called a well-being file). Add this information to their file, if it’s not already there:  * The child or youth’s name and birth date * The names and phone numbers of the caseworker, guardian ad litem, child advocate, and attorney * Medical information, immunization records, and medication records  * Written consent for providing medical care and emergency surgery, as authorized by a court order * A list of the child or youth’s personal belongings. Keep this list updated with any items they get while they live with you.   * The names, addresses, and phone numbers of emergency contacts * The names, addresses, and phone numbers for people who are allowed to have unsupervised access to the child or youth  * Any specific cultural, social, and emotional needs that the child or youth has * **Keep track of transportation expenses** on the [Caregiver Monthly Transportation Reimbursement form](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/forms?field_number_value=07-090&title=) and turn in complete forms to the caseworker to get reimbursed. * **Sign up for DCYF's online portal for managing foster care payments, and submit your request for your first monthly payment**. Learn more about foster care payments \[add link].  ### Other things you need to do  * **Tell the caseworker about any unplanned parent-child contact,** such as phone calls or unexpected visits. * **Share any concerns about the child or youth with the caseworker.** Examples include abuse, neglect, medical, behavioral, developmental, or educational issues. If the child or youth tells you something that worries you, do not ask follow-up questions yourself. Instead, listen carefully, let the child or youth know that you heard them, and report what they told you to the caseworker. * **Tell the caseworker the dates, times, and purposes of any appointments** that you set up for the child or youth. * **Work with DCYF to arrange subsidized child care if you plan to use child care.** The caseworker can give you a referral for a child care center that accepts DCYF payments. You cannot arrange or pay for child care on your own. This is to make sure that when the child is reunified with their family, the state can continue to pay for them to receive the same care. Connect with [Child Care Aware of Washington](https://childcareawarewa.org/) for help finding child care near you.  ## Other people who will contact you Different professionals will contact you about the child or youth. If you have any questions about who these individuals are and what information you can discuss with them, please ask the caseworker or their supervisor. [Learn more about who else you can contact for support](https://www.dcyf.wa.gov/publications-library/LIC_0081). Here are some of the professionals you might work with: * **Alliance CaRES:** Around the first week, you will hear from your CaRES mentor. Your mentor will answer your questions and support you as you care for a child or youth. The CaRES team can also provide referrals to other resources, including training and support groups. You can reach them at alliancecares@uw.edu or 206-221-4913. * **Someone from DCYF’s Child Health and Education Tracking (CHET) program:** A worker will contact you within 30 days of placement to help assess the child or youth’s medical needs.  * * If the CHET report identifies any medical needs for the child or youth, call Apple Health Core Connections at 1-844-354-9876 and ask for help with coordinating health care.  * For help with the child or youth’s education, contact your local [school district’s foster care liaison](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/student-success/accessopportunity-education/foster-care/foster-careliaisonsdcyf-contactsschool-contacts). * **Ongoing Mental Health (OMH) screener:** If the child or youth is 3 years old through 17 years old, a worker will contact you as part of the follow-up to the CHET assessment. They will check what emotional and behavioral health needs the child or youth has. You can request a referral for an OMH screener at any time. * **Child Advocates, Guardians ad Litem (GAL), and Court Appointed Special Advocates (CASA):** Their role is to advocate for children in court. The advocate may visit your home. * **The child or youth’s attorney:** Children and youth 12 and older may have an attorney. Children and youth whose parents’ rights have been terminated will have an attorney. The attorney may visit your home.
